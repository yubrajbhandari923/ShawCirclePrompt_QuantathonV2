What is PCA?

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a dimensionality reduction technique that simplifies large datasets by identifying the key patterns (principal components).

The Fundamentals ‚Äî How PCA Works

At its core, PCA finds a linear transformation that maximizes the variance of projected data.

This gives the directions of maximum variance i.e. your principal components.

PCA Approaches: Recursive vs Simultaneous

Recursive PCA (Deflation / One-Component-at-a-Time)

  • The recursive formulation extracts components sequentially, one after another
  • First, find direction v1 that maximizes variance:
v1 = arg max||v||=1 ||Xv||¬≤
  • Once v1 is found, we deflate the data by removing influence of that component:
X' = X ‚àí (Xv1)v1T
  • Then find v2 from residual X', and repeat recursively

Think of it like peeling quantum states off a superposition ‚Äî one eigenstate at a time.

This process is elegant, intuitive, and computationally efficient when k (number of components) is small.

Simultaneous PCA (Full Eigen-Decomposition)

  • Instead of deflating iteratively, we diagonalize the covariance matrix once:
C = VŒõVT

where V contains all eigenvectors and Œõ is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues

  • We then directly take the top k eigenvectors as the principal directions

This method is faster for dense datasets and can be parallelized ‚Äî perfect for quantum-style computations.

Quantum Annealing

Quantum Annealing is a quantum computing technique that finds the global minimum of an objective function by exploiting quantum tunneling and superposition.

üåä Quantum Tunneling

Unlike classical optimization that climbs over energy barriers, quantum systems can tunnel through barriers to escape local minima

‚ùÑÔ∏è Annealing Process

System starts in a superposition of all possible states and gradually "freezes" into the optimal solution

üéØ Ising Model

Problems are mapped to an Ising Hamiltonian where qubits represent binary variables (¬±1)

How it works:

  • Initialize system in quantum superposition (all states equally likely)
  • Gradually transition from transverse field to problem Hamiltonian
  • Quantum fluctuations help avoid local minima
  • System settles into ground state = optimal solution
